Beneath the Melting Ice

Magnetic Instability Threatening New Arctic Shipping Paths

Mujun Kim — mkim264@gmu.edu · Seungeun Choi — schoi72@gmu.edu
George Mason University Korea

The Changing Arctic

The Arctic is warming almost four times faster than the rest of the world. As temperatures rise, summer sea-ice continues to shrink, opening waters that were once locked by ice. These environmental changes are making the Northern Sea Route (NSR) and Northwest Passage (NWP) more accessible for seasonal navigation.

The Hidden Navigation Problem

Although melting ice is improving physical accessibility, the Arctic hides a unique challenge: magnetic-field instability. Near the poles, Earth's magnetic field dips steeply and shifts rapidly over short distances—conditions that can degrade GNSS accuracy and compass reliability.

How We Mapped the Invisible

To understand where navigation becomes unstable, we combined magnetic-field models with real Arctic shipping routes. Using the World Magnetic Model 2025 (WMM2025), we mapped high-gradient magnetic regions and overlaid them onto NSR and NWP maritime pathways.

What the Maps Reveal

Our analysis shows that both NSR and NWP intersect regions with high magnetic-field gradients. These are the areas where GNSS drift and compass deviation are most likely to occur, especially during geomagnetic disturbances.

Why It Matters

As Arctic trade increases, even small navigation errors can create major risks in narrow or ice-drifting channels. Ships crossing magnetic hotspots may require enhanced GNSS correction systems, multi-constellation receivers, inertial backup sensors, and real-time monitoring of geomagnetic activity.
