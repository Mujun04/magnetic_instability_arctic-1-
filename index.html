Beneath the Melting Ice: Magnetic Instability Threatening New Arctic Shipping Paths

Mujun Kim (mkim264@gmu.edu), Seungeun Choi (schoi72@gmu.edu)
George Mason University Korea

Introduction

Recent climate data shows that the Arctic is warming much faster than the global average. As the ocean becomes warmer, Arctic summer sea-ice continues to decline, opening hidden waters that were once blocked by ice. Because of this, shipping routes such as the Northern Sea Route and the Northwest Passage are becoming more accessible.

However, the Arctic is also an area where the Earth's magnetic field shifts dramatically, which can reduce the accuracy of satellite navigation systems (GPS/GNSS). This study visualizes magnetic hotspots and overlays them with Arctic shipping routes to identify potential areas of navigation instability.

Research Questions

As Arctic sea-ice loss opens major Arctic routes, can magnetic-field hotspot maps reveal which route segments are most vulnerable to GNSS inaccuracy?

Results

Methods

• Magnetic inclination and gradient values were retrieved from the World Magnetic Model (WMM2025), projected to a 2.5° × 2.5° grid over the Arctic region.
• Arctic shipping routes were digitized using public polar-route maps and manually georeferenced.
• To estimate possible GNSS instability zones, a simulation model was applied because actual AIS/GNSS error measurements in the Arctic remain restricted. Error probability increases with the steepness of magnetic gradients, following distance-decay functions commonly used in GNSS research.

Limitations

• Real GNSS error data is limited due to scarce satellite coverage and restricted commercial datasets.
• Figure 6 is simulation-based—not a direct measurement—but reflects realistic patterns described in Arctic navigation studies.
• Future versions can incorporate commercial AIS datasets or naval-grade GNSS logs to improve accuracy.

Key Terms

  • Declination: Angular difference between magnetic north and true north.
  • GNSS: Global Navigation Satellite System.
  • Magnetic Gradient: Rate of magnetic-field change across space.
  • NSR: Northern Sea Route.
  • NWP: Northwest Passage.
  • WMM: World Magnetic Model.

Conclusion

Our mapping indicates that some sections of the Northern Sea Route and Northwest Passage pass through areas of steep magnetic gradients, where GNSS accuracy may be reduced. As Arctic shipping increases due to sea-ice loss, updated magnetic models and real-time navigation monitoring will be essential to ensure safe travel through these regions.
