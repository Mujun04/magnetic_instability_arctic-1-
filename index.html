Mapping Magnetic Instability in the Arctic

Identifying GNSS-Vulnerable Sections of the Northern Sea Route & Northwest Passage

Mujun Kim · Seungeun Choi — George Mason University Korea

Abstract

The Arctic is warming rapidly, and as summer sea-ice melts, major shipping routes like the Northern Sea Route (NSR) and Northwest Passage (NWP) are opening for longer periods. While this creates new economic opportunities, it also exposes ships to a less visible but important risk: magnetic-field instability, which can weaken GNSS accuracy.

Read More ▼

We used the World Magnetic Model (WMM2025) to map where magnetic inclination and magnetic-field gradients change sharply across the Arctic. These hotspots are regions where Earth's magnetic field shifts quickly—something known to disturb GNSS signals, compass heading, and even radio communication.

By overlaying shipping routes on these hotspot maps, we found that several parts of both NSR and NWP run directly through areas of steep magnetic variation. Because real GNSS error logs in the Arctic are limited, we built a simulation-based GNSS error model that follows patterns from polar-navigation studies.

Our analysis shows that magnetic hotspots strongly align with areas of increased GNSS vulnerability. As Arctic traffic grows, safe navigation will require not only ice-free waters but also real-time awareness of magnetic-signal stability and space-weather conditions.

Introduction

As the Arctic warms almost four times faster than the rest of the world, summer sea-ice is shrinking rapidly. This change is opening long-blocked passages, allowing cargo ships to travel shorter distances between Asia, Europe, and North America. However, the Arctic's magnetic environment is extremely unstable, making reliable navigation—as important as avoiding sea-ice—much more challenging.
Sea-ice loss (Figure 2) means ships can now pass through the NSR and NWP more frequently. Yet these routes also cross regions where Earth's magnetic field tilts sharply downward or changes rapidly over short distances. These shifts can distort compass readings and weaken GNSS accuracy.

Research Questions

  • Which parts of the NSR and NWP overlap with magnetic-field hotspots?
  • Are these overlapping segments more vulnerable to GNSS inaccuracy?
  • Can magnetic-field maps help warn ships about unstable navigation zones?

Methods

We combined magnetic-field modeling, climate data, and geospatial visualization to evaluate GNSS-risk areas along Arctic shipping routes.

1. Data Collection

Shipping routes were extracted from maritime charts. Magnetic-field data came from WMM2025.

2. Magnetic Modeling

We calculated magnetic inclination and magnetic-field gradients—key indicators of GNSS instability.

3. Visualization

Cartopy and Matplotlib were used to create Arctic polar-projection hotspot maps.

4. GNSS Error Simulation

Because real Arctic GNSS logs are limited, we created a simulation that increases GNSS error where magnetic gradients are steep.

Results

Finding 1: Magnetic inclination becomes extremely steep near the polar regions, making navigation sensors less stable.
Finding 2: Magnetic-field gradient hotspots show where the magnetic field changes quickly—known triggers of GNSS inaccuracy.
Finding 3: Portions of both NSR and NWP overlap with these hotspots, meaning ships in these areas may face weaker GNSS performance.

Conclusion

Our maps show that some parts of the Northern Sea Route and Northwest Passage have larger GNSS position errors where they cross magnetic-field hotspots. As Arctic shipping increases, vessels traveling through these zones may need:
  • Upgraded GNSS correction tools
  • Alternative navigation systems (radar, inertial sensors)
  • Real-time monitoring of magnetic-field changes
  • Space-weather alerts to avoid peak geomagnetic disturbances
The future of Arctic shipping depends not only on ice-free seas but also on stable magnetic-signal environments.
