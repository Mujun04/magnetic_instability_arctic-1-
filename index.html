Beneath the Melting Ice:

Magnetic Instability Threatening New Arctic Shipping Paths

Mujun Kim (mkim264@gmu.edu), Seungeun Choi (schoi72@gmu.edu)

George Mason University Korea

Contact: mkim264@gmu.edu · schoi72@gmu.edu

Abstract

The Arctic is warming rapidly, and as summer sea-ice melts, major shipping routes such as the Northern Sea Route (NSR) and the Northwest Passage (NWP) are opening for longer periods. While this creates new opportunities, it also introduces a lesser-known challenge: magnetic-field instability, which can reduce the accuracy of GNSS navigation.

We analyzed magnetic inclination and magnetic-field gradient data from WMM2025 to identify hotspot regions across the Arctic. These hotspots represent areas where Earth’s magnetic field changes quickly, which can interfere with GNSS signals, compass heading, and even radio communication. When we overlaid Arctic shipping routes on the hotspot map, several segments of both the NSR and NWP passed directly through high-variability regions.

Because available GNSS degradation data in the Arctic is limited, we created a simulation-based GNSS error density model using known physical relationships from polar-navigation research. Our results show that GNSS vulnerability increases where magnetic gradients are strongest.

These findings suggest that as Arctic shipping expands, vessels will require not only ice-free waters but also improved GNSS correction systems and real-time awareness of magnetic-field changes and space-weather conditions.

Introduction

Recent climate observations show that the Arctic is warming much faster than the global average. As ocean temperatures rise, summer sea-ice continues to decline, revealing open-water areas that were once inaccessible. This transformation is making the NSR and NWP more viable as commercial shipping routes.
However, the Arctic also exhibits extreme magnetic instability. Magnetic inclination becomes very steep near the poles, and magnetic-field gradients shift unpredictably. These changes can reduce GNSS accuracy and affect navigation systems. Understanding these magnetic anomalies is becoming increasingly important as Arctic shipping grows.

Research Questions

As Arctic sea-ice loss opens new shipping routes, an important question arises: Are these accessible waters also regions where magnetic instability threatens GNSS accuracy?

  • Which parts of the NSR and NWP overlap with magnetic-field hotspots?
  • Do these hotspots align with regions where GNSS instability is likely?
  • Can magnetic hotspot maps help predict which segments require additional navigation caution?

Results

Magnetic inclination becomes extremely steep in high-latitude regions, creating conditions where heading sensors and magnetic compasses are less stable.
Magnetic-field gradients highlight areas where the magnetic field changes rapidly. These regions often coincide with known GNSS instability zones identified in previous polar-navigation studies.
Because real GNSS degradation data is sparse, we created a simulation-model that amplifies GNSS error where magnetic gradients are steep. The results show clear increases in GNSS uncertainty along segments of both the NSR and NWP.

Discussion

As sea-ice retreat improves the physical accessibility of Arctic waters, magnetic instability poses a new, less visible challenge. GNSS accuracy can degrade significantly in areas with steep magnetic-field gradients, especially during geomagnetic disturbances. These navigation uncertainties are particularly important in harsh Arctic environments where visibility may be limited and alternative navigation tools are harder to rely on.

Conclusion

Our maps show that some parts of the NSR and NWP have increased GNSS position errors where they overlap magnetic-field hotspots. Ships traveling through these regions may require upgraded GNSS corrections, multi-constellation receivers, or alternative navigation technologies. As Arctic shipping expands, real-time monitoring of magnetic-field variations and space weather will become essential for safe navigation.
