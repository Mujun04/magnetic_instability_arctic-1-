Arctic Navigation Risk Dashboard

GNSS Vulnerability · Magnetic Hotspots · Sea-Ice Change

Mujun Kim — mkim264@gmu.edu · Seungeun Choi — schoi72@gmu.edu
George Mason University Korea

Abstract

Rapid Arctic warming is opening the Northern Sea Route (NSR) and Northwest Passage (NWP) for longer shipping seasons. But newly accessible waters also intersect regions of extreme magnetic instability—areas where GNSS accuracy, compass reliability, and radio communication become less stable. This project evaluates how magnetic-field hotspots overlap with emerging Arctic shipping routes.

Read More ▼

Using the World Magnetic Model (WMM2025), we mapped magnetic inclination and field gradients to identify Arctic magnetic hotspots. These gradients highlight regions where the magnetic field changes rapidly across short distances. When NSR and NWP routes were overlaid on the hotspot map, multiple segments crossed zones known for unstable GNSS performance and compass drift.

Because real GNSS degradation data in the Arctic is limited, we applied a physics-informed simulation to estimate error density patterns. The simulation amplifies GNSS drift in areas with steep gradients, consistent with peer-reviewed polar-navigation studies.

Our findings reveal that even as sea-ice retreat improves physical accessibility, invisible magnetic hazards may introduce new navigation risks—especially during geomagnetic disturbances.

Introduction

The Arctic is warming nearly four times faster than the global average. As summer sea-ice retreats, previously blocked waters along the NSR and NWP are becoming navigable, reducing travel distance between Asia, Europe, and North America.
Declining Arctic sea-ice (Figure 2) is creating new seasonal navigation windows. This transformation enables economic opportunities but also increases the need for reliable navigation systems in extreme environments.
However, the Arctic is one of Earth’s least magnetically stable regions. Steep magnetic inclination and rapidly shifting magnetic gradients complicate GNSS guidance, compass heading, and radio communication— creating invisible risks along Arctic shipping corridors.

Research Question

As Arctic sea-ice loss opens the Northern Sea Route and Northwest Passage, can magnetic-field hotspot maps identify which segments are most vulnerable to GNSS inaccuracy?

Key Terms

  • Magnetic Inclination — Angle of magnetic-field lines dipping into Earth; extremely steep near the poles.
  • Magnetic Gradient — How rapidly the magnetic field changes over distance; strong gradients destabilize compasses & GNSS.
  • GNSS Drift — Gradual wandering or offset of satellite-based positions due to unstable signal or magnetic interference.

Results

Magnetic inclination becomes extremely steep in high-latitude regions, creating unstable compass behaviors and contributing to GNSS inaccuracy.
Magnetic gradients identify zones where the magnetic field changes rapidly—areas correlated with satellite-based navigation drift.
Due to sparse real GNSS degradation logs, a physics-based simulation was constructed. Results show higher GNSS uncertainty on segments of NSR and NWP that intersect magnetic hotspots.

Datasets & Tools

This project integrates multiple geophysical and climate datasets used in Arctic research. Global temperature anomalies were sourced from the NOAA & Berkeley Earth temperature index. Sea-ice extent trends were taken from the National Snow and Ice Data Center (NSIDC). Arctic shipping-route geometry (NSR & NWP) follows publicly available ESA maritime data.

Magnetic inclination and magnetic-field gradients were computed using the World Magnetic Model (WMM2025) provided by NOAA and the British Geological Survey. Figures were generated using Python (Cartopy, Matplotlib) and processed into a unified dashboard-style interface.

GNSS error density is a physics-based simulation referencing peer-reviewed polar-navigation studies, used to approximate expected GNSS drift under different magnetic-gradient conditions.

Limitations

Real GNSS degradation measurements in the Arctic are limited due to sparse ship traffic, harsh environmental conditions, and restricted access to vessel navigation logs. As a result, our GNSS-error map incorporates simulations based on known magnetic-field physics and prior polar-navigation research.

Higher-resolution GNSS drift measurements, seasonal magnetic-field updates, and real-time vessel AIS logs would significantly improve map accuracy.

Why It Matters

As Arctic shipping increases due to climate warming, even small navigation errors can lead to dangerous situations such as grounding, collisions, or deviations into hazardous ice fields. Many Arctic regions have minimal rescue infrastructure, making reliable navigation systems critical.

Identifying magnetically vulnerable segments of the NSR and NWP allows vessels to prepare with improved GNSS correction systems, multi-constellation receivers, redundancy sensors, and real-time monitoring of magnetic-field variations and geomagnetic storms.

Conclusion

Our analysis shows that key segments of the Northern Sea Route and Northwest Passage intersect magnetic-field hotspots where GNSS drift becomes more likely. As Arctic navigation expands, understanding these invisible magnetic risks will be essential for shipping safety, route planning, and future Arctic maritime policy.
